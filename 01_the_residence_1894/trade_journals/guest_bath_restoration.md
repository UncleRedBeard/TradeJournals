# Trade Journal: Guest Bath Restoration

## The Challenge

Document the guest bath work as a preservation-minded room restoration inside the 1894 residence: what existing conditions were uncovered, what old-house constraints shaped the repair, and how modern bathroom function could be integrated without erasing the room's historic relationship to the house.

## Historic And Building Context

The guest bath belongs in the residence archive as a compact but trade-heavy room project. Bathroom work often exposes the collision between historic framing, old finishes, plumbing routes, ventilation, electrical needs, waterproofing, and modern fixtures, so this journal should preserve the reasoning behind each repair and replacement decision.

## Craftsmanship Execution

Capture the work by phase and trade:

- existing condition and pre-work documentation
- demolition, opening, and investigation
- framing, subfloor, wall, ceiling, and substrate repair
- plumbing, electrical, ventilation, and waterproofing integration
- fixture, trim, tile, paint, and finish installation
- final room assembly, function testing, and quality review

## Materials And Tools

Record the specific tools, materials, and reference choices used during the work. Include details such as plumbing components, waterproofing products, tile or wall finishes, primers, paints, trim profiles, fasteners, sealants, ventilation parts, and any old-house repair methods that affected the final outcome.

## Tradeoffs Avoided Or Accepted

Explain where preservation mattered most. Note what was repaired instead of replaced, what had to be modernized for bathroom performance or code, and how those decisions balanced durability, moisture control, serviceability, and historic character.

## Journal Entries

### 2024-01-15 To 2024-04-23 - Guest Bath Surface And Fixture Work

Source: Flickr album [Home Reno - Guest Bath](https://www.flickr.com/photos/boocher/albums/72177720314827829/) and first-pass photo review.

The guest bath album records a compact but trade-heavy room moving from existing condition into active repair, prep, finish, and fixture assembly. The early photos preserve beadboard/wainscot damage, tub and vanity placement, old floor tile, plumbing access, and existing paint. The middle sequence shows the room under active surface preparation: scuffed paint, patched walls, protected fixtures, exposed plumbing, lighting work, and repair around the beadboard and base. The later sequence captures the room returning toward service with lighter wall finishes, black lighting and tub finish, vanity/sink assembly, and the antique dresser being adapted as the sink base.

First-pass interpretation:

- The album is strongest as evidence of finish sequencing and fixture integration in a compact old-house bath.
- The existing beadboard/wainscot and old tile floor are important continuity elements.
- The antique dresser vanity and vessel sink are a key craft/fitment problem rather than a simple fixture swap.
- The room stays visually connected to the house through retained trim, windows, beadboard, clawfoot tub, and small-format floor tile.
